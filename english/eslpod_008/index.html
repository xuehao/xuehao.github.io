No.8 Personal letters

发表于 2019-07-01 归类

Today's podcast is going to feature a person, a woman who's writing an email to a friend.

And we've asked her to read this email to us. So we can get a better idea about how personal emails and personal messages differ in English from formal messages.

Let's listen it now to see what new expressions and words we can learn from this personal email.

Hi Julia,

I'm sitting here in front of the computer in our apartment, writing this email to you.

It feels so wired not having you here. You were my roommate for four years after all. I really miss having my best friend here, but enough of feeling sorry for myself. I'm so happy to hear that you're getting settled in New York City.

I'm trying to imagine what it's like for you. First, getting your dream job. Then, moving to an amazing city. And starting a whole new life. I'm really glad to hear that your cross country drive to New York was a good one. There were no problems, no breakdowns, and you even made it in good time. I'm very very happy to hear that the moving van made it there without any major catastrophes. I can't believe they made it there on time. And they even didn't break anything. What a miracle!

Well, I hope that everything else goes well this next week while you're getting used to a new city before starting your new job. Are you excited about the job yet? It's so great that you'll be able to do what you've always wanted to, working as a writer for one of the best newspapers in a country. I always knew you could do it. You're always so focused and work so hard. You really deserve it. I can't wait to start reading your articles in the newspaper.

Well, thing here are pretty much as you left it. Same old same old. My schedule at the restaurant is still the same--I'm working weekends. I'm not thrilled about that as you know. But I do love my job, being a chef is great. But there's one major drawback. I'm starting to notice that I'm getting so fat. I have to stop eating so many of my own dishes. I decided today to do something about it. I joined the gym down the street. It's the one, three blocks down, near that video store. Do you remember it? I even went out today and got new workout clothes and a pair of new jogging shoes. Now I have absolutely no excuses. All I have to do is to get off my butt and go workout. I'm so incredibly lazy. You know what a couch potato I am, but not anymore. I plan to get up each morning and workout before I head off to work. That means actually getting up before 10 a.m. Now, that's going to be a challenge. I'm going to start on Monday. We'll see how that goes. Wish me luck.

Ok. That's off now. Write me back soon. I'm anxious to hear how things are going. Talk to you soon.

Jessica

Explanation:

  • to feel sorry for yourself

    to pity yourself, to think that your situation is worse than everyone else

    This is something we would only say however to a friend, family member, or someone that you knew well. You wouldn't say that to your boss.

  • dream job

    Using the term "dream" in front of a noun means it's perfect or ideal.

  • breakdown

    A car or an automobile has problems and stops running, we say that it breaks down. The term "breakdown" would be the noun of that.

  • Same old, same old.

    Informal It means that nothing has changed, an informal one that we use among friends and family.

    It also implies a certain boredom, a certain wanting for some change but not being able to produce that change.

  • major drawback

    a negative part of some situation

    We use it to talk about something that has good and bad things.

  • to get off my butt

    Informal meaning we need to get moving, we need to get going, we need to stop doing what we suppose to do.

  • couch potato

    Informal meaning someone who is lazy, someone who sits around all day, doing nothing, watching TV or playing games.
